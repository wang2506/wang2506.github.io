Research

Overview

My research brings distributed and federated learning into the wireless physical and network layer, with the goal of making learning a native capability of 6G/NextG systems rather than a black box bolted on top. Getting machine intelligence to work over real networks — where devices, data, channels, and objectives all differ — is a foundational problem I've spent my Ph.D. and postdoc developing tools for. I'm now applying those tools where I think they matter most: inside the communication system itself, in problems like beamforming, semantic communications, and resource allocation for future wireless networks.

Research Thread

Learning-Native Wireless Communications (6G / NextG)

Most machine learning for wireless treats the communication system as fixed and the model as a black box. I take the opposite view: that the structure of a wireless problem — its physics, its optimization landscape, its distributed nature — should shape the learning architecture itself. My work here designs learning methods that are native to the communication system rather than generic, using distributed and unrolled optimization to build new physical- and network-layer capabilities.

A representative direction is distributed Airy beamforming via federated network unrolling (ongoing work), where beamforming optimization is cast as a learned, unrollable procedure distributed across the network — a concrete bridge between the distributed-learning tools I've developed and the wireless PHY. Related work includes holographic beamforming for semantic communications, which explores how learning can reshape what a communication system chooses to transmit in the first place.

Research Thread

Distributed & Federated Learning over Heterogeneous Networks

Before machine intelligence can live inside a wireless system, it has to survive the network: unreliable links, limited communication budgets, and devices whose hardware, data, and goals all differ. This heterogeneity is something standard distributed and federated approaches simplify away, but that realistic edge, fog, and IoT deployments cannot avoid. My doctoral and postdoctoral work develops the theory and algorithms that make distributed learning work under these conditions — treating it as foundational infrastructure for learning-native networks rather than an end in itself.

This thread spans network-aware optimization of distributed learning, device sampling and resource optimization for heterogeneous federated learning, online and personalized learning over multi-tiered networks, and server placement for vertical FL in dynamic edge/fog networks. More recent work extends these methods toward serverless and decentralized settings, and toward the robustness of learning-based wireless components — for example, defending federated signal classifiers against evasion attacks.

Recent & Ongoing

Recent & Ongoing Directions

Alongside these two threads, I work with students on emerging directions at the edge of learning and wireless systems: communication-efficient multimodal federated learning, controllable LLM offloading and routing for edge deployment, federated unlearning for generative models, and low-cost RF fingerprinting via TinyML. Several of these are led by undergraduate and graduate researchers I mentor.
